**July 15: Finalizing PCB**

After hours of tinkering and iterating on my design in KiCad, I think I finally finished CloudPad's PCB! This is my first time actually designing my own PCB, and so far I think it looks great. As of right now, CloudPad will just have 9 programmable switches arranges in a 3x3 grid, but I'm planning on adding some more stuff (like a screen, rotary encoder, or LEDs) in a future version.

Schematic design at first was confusing, but I got the hang of it quickly thanks to the research I did on KiCad's tools. The Electric Rule Checker (ERC) was a lifesaver for me! Without it, my PCB would have failed tremendously.

<img width="1030" height="842" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/a6fe880e-1621-4dfe-b51a-dbd0008ceb03" />

The hardest part about this project for me was setting up the diode matrix. I had never even heard of one before making the PCB, so it was a challenge understanding and applying one to my design. In the end, I think it was a nice incorporation that really added to my experience creating CloudPad.

Finally, actually designing the PCB was another obstacle because it's super tedious and easy to make mistakes with. My first iteration failed because there were overlapping components. My second failed because there was an issue with the tracks. Placing the tracks is sort of like a puzzle because you can't overlap them, but you still want them to look nice and use less copper.

<img width="1050" height="1318" alt="image" src="https://github.com/user-attachments/assets/47118aec-e41b-4544-a023-264573ed4ec6" />

It was super satisfying to finally visualize my PCB with the 3D view. I think it looks great and it _appears_ functional, but I'll double-check everything and prototype with a breadboard first. Overall, although it was challenging, my first experience with KiCad was rewarding and fun!
